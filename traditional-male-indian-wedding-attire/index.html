Traditional Male Indian Wedding Attire A Guide To Dressing For The Big Day

Written by Oliver Sep 23, 2023 · 5 min read

Indian weddings are known for their vibrant colors, elaborate decor, and of course, the stunning attire. For men, choosing the right traditional outfit can be a daunting task. From dhotis to sherwanis, there are many options to choose from, and it can be overwhelming to know where to start.

If you're a groom, family member, or guest attending an Indian wedding, you may be feeling the pressure to dress appropriately and in line with the cultural norms. After all, weddings are a time for celebration, and dressing up is a way to show respect and honor the occasion.

In this guide, we'll explore the different types of traditional male Indian wedding attire, their meanings, and how to choose the right one for you.

What is Traditional Male Indian Wedding Attire?

Traditional male Indian wedding attire includes a range of outfits that are worn during pre-wedding, wedding, and post-wedding ceremonies. These outfits vary based on the region, religion, and personal preferences of the wearer.

Some of the most popular traditional male Indian wedding attire includes:

  • Sherwani
  • Achkan
  • Kurta
  • Dhoti
  • Bandhgala

My Personal Experience with Traditional Male Indian Wedding Attire

As a South Asian who has attended numerous Indian weddings, I have had the opportunity to wear various traditional male Indian wedding attire. One of my favorite outfits is the sherwani, which is a long coat-like garment worn over a kurta and churidar pants. The sherwani is often heavily embellished with embroidery or beading, and it's typically worn with a turban and jootis (traditional Indian shoes).

Another outfit that I've worn is the dhoti, which is a long rectangular piece of cloth that is draped around the waist and legs. The dhoti is typically worn with a kurta or a sherwani and can be accessorized with a shawl or a dupatta. While the dhoti can be challenging to wear, it's a great option for those who want to try something different and unique.

Choosing the Right Traditional Male Indian Wedding Attire

When it comes to choosing the right traditional male Indian wedding attire, there are a few factors to consider:

  • Region: Different regions in India have their own unique style of traditional attire. For example, the sherwani is more commonly worn in North India, while the mundu (similar to a dhoti) is popular in South India.
  • Religion: Depending on the religion of the wedding, there may be specific attire requirements. For example, Sikh grooms may wear a turban and a churidar or pajama, while Hindu grooms may wear a sehra (a veil) over their face.
  • Personal Style: While it's important to consider the cultural norms and expectations, don't be afraid to inject your personal style into your outfit. Whether it's a bold color or a unique accessory, adding a personal touch can make your outfit stand out.

Types of Traditional Male Indian Wedding Attire

Sherwani: The sherwani is a long coat-like garment that is typically worn over a kurta and churidar pants. It's often heavily embellished with embroidery or beading and is worn with a turban and jootis.

Achkan: The achkan is similar to the sherwani but is shorter in length and has a more fitted silhouette. It's typically worn with a churidar or a dhoti and can be accessorized with a dupatta.

Kurta: The kurta is a long tunic-like garment that is typically worn with pajama pants or churidar pants. It can be worn with or without a jacket and is often accessorized with a dupatta.

Dhoti: The dhoti is a long rectangular piece of cloth that is draped around the waist and legs. It's typically worn with a kurta or a sherwani and can be accessorized with a shawl or a dupatta.

Bandhgala: The bandhgala is a jacket-like garment that is typically worn with a kurta or a shirt and pants. It has a high collar and is often embellished with buttons or embroidery.

How to Wear Traditional Male Indian Wedding Attire

Wearing traditional male Indian wedding attire can be a bit intimidating, especially if you're not used to wearing this type of clothing. Here are a few tips to help you look and feel your best:

  • Fit: Make sure your outfit fits well and is comfortable to wear. Ill-fitting clothing can be distracting and can detract from your overall look.
  • Accessories: Don't forget to accessorize your outfit with a turban, shawl, or jootis. These small details can make a big difference in your overall look.
  • Color: Indian weddings are known for their vibrant colors, so don't be afraid to wear something bold and colorful. However, if you're not comfortable with bright colors, you can opt for something more muted.

Question and Answer

Q: Do I have to wear traditional male Indian wedding attire to a wedding?

A: While it's not mandatory, it's highly recommended to dress in traditional attire as it shows respect to the cultural traditions and the couple getting married.

Q: Can I wear a suit to an Indian wedding?

A: While a suit is technically allowed, it's not recommended as it may be seen as disrespectful. Traditional attire is the preferred choice for men attending an Indian wedding.

Q: Can I wear jeans to an Indian wedding?

A: No, jeans are not appropriate attire for an Indian wedding. Traditional attire or formal wear is recommended.

Q: Can I wear a turban even if I'm not Sikh?

A: Yes, anyone can wear a turban as it's a symbol of respect and honor. However, it's important to learn the proper way to tie a turban, and it's recommended to seek guidance from someone who is knowledgeable in this area.

Conclusion of Traditional Male Indian Wedding Attire

Choosing the right traditional male Indian wedding attire can be a challenging task, but with a little research and preparation, you can find the perfect outfit for the big day. Remember to consider the region, religion, and personal style when choosing your outfit, and don't be afraid to inject your own personality into your look. With the right outfit and accessories, you'll be sure to turn heads and make a lasting impression.
